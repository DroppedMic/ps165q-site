The challenge

Your teachers, students, and families are already using AI. Your school's policy is the only thing that isn't.

  1. 01

    Vendors are moving faster than your procurement cycle.

    By the time a tool reaches your desk for review, three more have been adopted classroom-by-classroom — usually with terms no district lawyer would sign.

  2. 02

    "AI literacy" alone won't hold up to a school board.

    Leaders need defensible frameworks: who gets to use what, when student work counts as their own, and what privacy guarantees actually mean in practice.

  3. 03

    The strongest leaders aren't waiting for state guidance.

    They're drafting their own — and Lumen is where they do it, alongside peers carrying the same weight, with faculty who've done it before.

What you'll leave with

Six working artifacts. Not six more PDFs to read.

Every module ends with a deliverable you can hand to a board, a union, or a parent meeting on Monday. Drafts get peer-reviewed inside the cohort and stress-tested by faculty.

/ 01

A district-grade AI use policy you've actually argued through.

Acceptable use, student data, equity guardrails, escalation paths — each clause defended in front of the cohort before it touches your board.

/ 02

A vendor evaluation rubric.

Score new tools against the same questions every time, in 30 minutes flat.

/ 03

A 90-day rollout map.

Pilot, evaluate, expand — with the people, timelines, and budget lines named.

/ 04

A staff development arc.

Three PD sessions, fully designed, that respect teacher time.

/ 05

A family communication kit.

Letters, FAQs, and meeting decks ready for the parents in the room who've read the worst headlines.

/ 06

A measurement plan.

How you'll know — at 30, 60, 180 days — whether any of this is actually working.

Eight weeks · curriculum

A sequence built by school leaders, for the decisions you're already being asked to make.

Two live sessions per week, plus a working session with your cohort triad. Faculty office hours every Friday. Asynchronous reading kept under 90 minutes.

  1. Foundations

    What an LLM actually does — and what it doesn't.

    A working mental model strong enough to handle vendor pitches, parent questions, and bad press. We'll cover tokens, training data, hallucination, and where the real risks live.

    Mental models Risk framing
  2. Policy

    Drafting the acceptable-use policy.

    Line-by-line, in the language your district actually uses. We red-team three drafts from prior cohorts and rebuild one of them together.

    Policy drafting Red-team Board readiness
  3. Privacy

    Student data, FERPA, and what vendor contracts don't say.

    A privacy counsel walks through three real contracts. You'll learn which clauses to delete, which to negotiate, and which to walk away from.

    FERPA Vendor terms Data minimization
  4. Pedagogy

    Assessment in the age of generative tools.

    Detection doesn't scale. Instead, we look at task design, oral defenses, in-class evidence, and what "the student's own work" should mean now.

    Assessment design Academic integrity
  5. Equity

    Who gets the good tools — and who pays the cost.

    Access disparities, bias in outputs, English-learner implications, and the hard call on devices and home use.

    Equity audit EL & SPED
  6. Operations

    AI for leaders, not for show.

    The handful of tools that actually save principals and central-office staff time — scheduling, parent communication, IEP drafting, board prep — used live.

    Workflow design Time recovered
  7. Staff

    PD your teachers won't dread.

    Three sessions, ninety minutes each, with grade-band variants. Built around teacher problems, not vendor demos.

    PD design Change management
  8. Capstone

    Defending your plan in front of the room.

    Each leader presents their 90-day rollout to two faculty and three peers. You leave with signed feedback and a board-ready deck.

    Capstone Board deck

Questions

The ones we get the most often. And the honest answers.

I'm already using ChatGPT. Why do I need this?
This program isn't about prompt skills. It's about what you put in writing for your community — policies, communications, decisions — once thousands of students and staff are already using these tools. Tool fluency is the floor, not the ceiling.
How much work outside of live sessions?
About four hours per week, total, including two live sessions. Reading is capped at 90 minutes. The work is concrete: drafting, reviewing, rewriting — not abstract reflection.
Will this work for a small or rural district?
Yes. Roughly a third of every cohort comes from districts under 5,000 students. The frameworks scale down cleanly — and being in a triad with leaders from very different contexts is, candidly, the part graduates rave about.
Do you offer scholarships?
We reserve six full-tuition seats and twelve partial-tuition seats per cohort, prioritized for Title I districts and first-time school leaders. The application includes a brief scholarship section — no separate process.
Can Title II or ESSER funds be used?
Title II almost always. ESSER timelines vary by state — we'll send the line-item documentation your finance office will ask for as soon as you apply.
What if I miss a session?
Sessions are recorded, and your triad will catch you up. We ask leaders to commit to at least 12 of 16 live sessions — under that, the cohort dynamic doesn't hold.
